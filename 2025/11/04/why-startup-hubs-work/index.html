创业中心为何能成功

Why Startup Hubs Work

创业中心为何能成功

原文链接: https://paulgraham.com/hubs.html

October 2011

If you look at a list of US cities sorted by population, the number of successful startups per capita varies by orders of magnitude. Somehow it’s as if most places were sprayed with startupicide.若查看美国按人口排序的城市名单，会发现各城市人均成功创业公司数量相差极大。不知为何，多数地区仿佛被喷洒了 “创业杀手”（注：作者造词，指抑制创业的因素）。

I wondered about this for years. I could see the average town was like a roach motel for startup ambitions: smart, ambitious people went in, but no startups came out. But I was never able to figure out exactly what happened inside the motel—exactly what was killing all the potential startups. [1]多年来我一直对此感到困惑。我发现普通城镇就像 “蟑螂旅馆”（注：比喻只进不出的地方），吞噬着创业抱负：聪明且有野心的人踏入其中，却没有创业公司能从中诞生。但我始终无法弄清楚 “旅馆” 内部究竟发生了什么 —— 究竟是什么扼杀了所有潜在的创业公司。[1]

A couple weeks ago I finally figured it out. I was framing the question wrong. The problem is not that most towns kill startups. It’s that death is the default for startups, and most towns don’t save them. Instead of thinking of most places as being sprayed with startupicide, it’s more accurate to think of startups as all being poisoned, and a few places being sprayed with the antidote.几周前我终于找到了答案。此前我一直用错了思考框架。问题不在于多数城镇会扼杀创业公司，而在于 “失败” 本就是创业公司的默认结局，且多数城镇无法挽救它们。与其认为多数地区被喷洒了 “创业杀手”，不如将所有创业公司视为 “中毒者”，而少数地区则被喷洒了 “解药”，这种说法更为准确。

Startups in other places are just doing what startups naturally do: fail. The real question is, what’s saving startups in places like Silicon Valley? [2]其他地区的创业公司只是在做创业公司本能会做的事：失败。真正的问题是，像硅谷这样的地方，究竟是什么在挽救创业公司？[2]

Environment

环境因素

I think there are two components to the antidote: being in a place where startups are the cool thing to do, and chance meetings with people who can help you. And what drives them both is the number of startup people around you.我认为 “解药” 包含两个要素：一是身处一个 “创业是件很酷的事” 的环境，二是能偶然遇到可提供帮助的人。而驱动这两个要素的关键，在于你身边 “创业相关人士” 的数量。

The first component is particularly helpful in the first stage of a startup’s life, when you go from merely having an interest in starting a company to actually doing it. It’s quite a leap to start a startup. It’s an unusual thing to do. But in Silicon Valley it seems normal. [3]第一个要素在创业公司的初始阶段尤为重要，也就是当你从 “只是有创业兴趣” 转变为 “真正付诸行动” 的阶段。创办一家创业公司是巨大的跨越，并非寻常之举，但在硅谷，这似乎是件很正常的事。[3]

In most places, if you start a startup, people treat you as if you’re unemployed. People in the Valley aren’t automatically impressed with you just because you’re starting a company, but they pay attention. Anyone who’s been here any amount of time knows not to default to skepticism, no matter how inexperienced you seem or how unpromising your idea sounds at first, because they’ve all seen inexperienced founders with unpromising sounding ideas who a few years later were billionaires.在多数地区，若你创办创业公司，人们会把你当成失业者。硅谷的人不会仅仅因为你在创业就对你另眼相看，但他们会关注你。任何在硅谷待过一段时间的人都知道，不应默认持怀疑态度 —— 无论你看起来多缺乏经验，无论你的想法起初听起来多没前景。因为他们都见过这样的案例：曾经缺乏经验、想法看似无望的创始人，几年后却成了亿万富翁。

Having people around you care about what you’re doing is an extraordinarily powerful force. Even the most willful people are susceptible to it. About a year after we started Y Combinator I said something to a partner at a well known VC firm that gave him the (mistaken) impression I was considering starting another startup. He responded so eagerly that for about half a second I found myself considering doing it.身边有人关注你所做的事，这是一种极具力量的推动力，即便最固执的人也会受其影响。在我们创办 Y Combinator（注：知名创业孵化器）约一年后，我曾和一家知名风投公司的合伙人聊起某事，让他产生了一个（错误的）印象：我在考虑再创办一家创业公司。他的反应极为热切，以至于那一瞬间我真的开始认真考虑这件事。

In most other cities, the prospect of starting a startup just doesn’t seem real. In the Valley it’s not only real but fashionable. That no doubt causes a lot of people to start startups who shouldn’t. But I think that’s ok. Few people are suited to running a startup, and it’s very hard to predict beforehand which are (as I know all too well from being in the business of trying to predict beforehand), so lots of people starting startups who shouldn’t is probably the optimal state of affairs. As long as you’re at a point in your life when you can bear the risk of failure, the best way to find out if you’re suited to running a startup is to try it.在其他多数城市，“创办创业公司” 这件事看起来就不真实。但在硅谷，它不仅真实，还很时髦。这无疑会让很多本不适合创业的人也投身其中，但我认为这并无不妥。适合经营创业公司的人本就不多，且提前预测谁适合更是难上加难（我身处这一行，对此深有体会）。因此，让很多本不适合的人尝试创业，或许是最佳状态。只要你正处于人生中能承担失败风险的阶段，想知道自己是否适合经营创业公司，最好的方法就是去尝试。

Chance

偶然因素

The second component of the antidote is chance meetings with people who can help you. This force works in both phases: both in the transition from the desire to start a startup to starting one, and the transition from starting a company to succeeding. The power of chance meetings is more variable than people around you caring about startups, which is like a sort of background radiation that affects everyone equally, but at its strongest it is far stronger.“解药” 的第二个要素是 “偶然遇到能提供帮助的人”。这种力量在两个阶段都发挥作用：一是从 “有创业意愿” 到 “实际创业” 的过渡阶段，二是从 “创办公司” 到 “取得成功” 的过渡阶段。相较于 “身边人关注创业” 这种像 “背景辐射” 一样平等影响所有人的力量，偶然相遇的影响力更具不确定性，但在其最强时，威力要大得多。

Chance meetings produce miracles to compensate for the disasters that characteristically befall startups. In the Valley, terrible things happen to startups all the time, just like they do to startups everywhere. The reason startups are more likely to make it here is that great things happen to them too. In the Valley, lightning has a sign bit.偶然相遇能创造奇迹，弥补创业公司常遭遇的灾难。在硅谷，创业公司总会遇到糟糕的事，这和其他地方的创业公司并无二致。但硅谷的创业公司更有可能成功，原因在于它们也会遇到好事。可以说，在硅谷，“好运” 是带有 “标识” 的（注：比喻好运更易降临）。

For example, you start a site for college students and you decide to move to the Valley for the summer to work on it. And then on a random suburban street in Palo Alto you happen to run into Sean Parker, who understands the domain really well because he started a similar startup himself, and also knows all the investors. And moreover has advanced views, for 2004, on founders retaining control of their companies.举个例子：你创办了一个面向大学生的网站，并决定夏天搬到硅谷专注运营它。然后在帕洛阿尔托（注：硅谷核心城市之一）一条普通的郊区街道上，你偶然遇到了肖恩・帕克（注：Napster 和 Facebook 早期高管，创业领域资深人士）。他对这个领域了如指掌 —— 因为他自己就创办过类似的创业公司，还认识所有投资人。此外，在 2004 年那个年代，他就对 “创始人保留公司控制权” 有着超前的见解。

You can’t say precisely what the miracle will be, or even for sure that one will happen. The best one can say is: if you’re in a startup hub, unexpected good things will probably happen to you, especially if you deserve them.你无法准确预测会遇到什么样的奇迹，甚至无法确定奇迹是否会发生。但可以肯定的是：若你身处创业中心，意外的好事很可能会降临到你身上，尤其是当你配得上这些好事时。

I bet this is true even for startups we fund. Even with us working to make things happen for them on purpose rather than by accident, the frequency of helpful chance meetings in the Valley is so high that it’s still a significant increment on what we can deliver.我敢说，即便是我们投资的创业公司，情况也是如此。即便我们刻意为它们创造机会，而非依赖偶然，硅谷中 “有帮助的偶然相遇” 频率依然极高，这仍是我们所能提供资源之外的重要补充。

Chance meetings play a role like the role relaxation plays in having ideas. Most people have had the experience of working hard on some problem, not being able to solve it, giving up and going to bed, and then thinking of the answer in the shower in the morning. What makes the answer appear is letting your thoughts drift a bit—and thus drift off the wrong path you’d been pursuing last night and onto the right one adjacent to it.偶然相遇的作用，就像 “放松” 在 “产生灵感” 过程中所起的作用。多数人都有过这样的经历：努力解决某个问题却无果，只好放弃去睡觉，第二天早上洗澡时却突然想到了答案。答案之所以会出现，是因为你让思绪稍微 “飘移” 了 —— 从昨晚执着的错误路径上移开，飘到了旁边正确的路径上。

Chance meetings let your acquaintance drift in the same way taking a shower lets your thoughts drift. The critical thing in both cases is that they drift just the right amount. The meeting between Larry Page and Sergey Brin was a good example. They let their acquaintance drift, but only a little; they were both meeting someone they had a lot in common with.偶然相遇能让你的 “人脉圈” 发生飘移，就像洗澡能让你的思绪飘移一样。两种情况的关键在于，“飘移” 的程度恰到好处。拉里・佩奇（注：谷歌联合创始人）和谢尔盖・布林（注：谷歌联合创始人）的相遇就是个好例子。他们的人脉圈发生了飘移，但幅度不大 —— 他们遇到的是与自己有很多共同点的人。

For Larry Page the most important component of the antidote was Sergey Brin, and vice versa. The antidote is people. It’s not the physical infrastructure of Silicon Valley that makes it work, or the weather, or anything like that. Those helped get it started, but now that the reaction is self-sustaining what drives it is the people.对拉里・佩奇而言，“解药” 中最重要的要素是谢尔盖・布林，反之亦然。“解药” 的核心是人。让硅谷成功的，不是它的实体基础设施，也不是天气或其他类似因素。这些因素确实帮助硅谷起步，但如今其发展已形成自我循环，驱动这一切的核心是人。

Many observers have noticed that one of the most distinctive things about startup hubs is the degree to which people help one another out, with no expectation of getting anything in return. I’m not sure why this is so. Perhaps it’s because startups are less of a zero sum game than most types of business; they are rarely killed by competitors. Or perhaps it’s because so many startup founders have backgrounds in the sciences, where collaboration is encouraged.许多观察人士注意到，创业中心最显著的特征之一，是人们会主动互相帮助，且不期望任何回报。我不确定背后的原因是什么。或许是因为创业公司不像多数行业那样属于 “零和博弈”（注：一方获利必然导致另一方损失），它们很少被竞争对手击垮；又或许是因为很多创业公司创始人拥有科学领域背景，而该领域本身就鼓励合作。

A large part of YC’s function is to accelerate that process. We’re a sort of Valley within the Valley, where the density of people working on startups and their willingness to help one another are both artificially amplified.Y Combinator 的很大一部分职能就是加速这一过程。我们就像是 “硅谷中的硅谷”—— 在这里，创业相关人士的密度以及他们互相帮助的意愿，都被人为地放大了。

Numbers

人数因素

Both components of the antidote—an environment that encourages startups, and chance meetings with people who help you—are driven by the same underlying cause: the number of startup people around you. To make a startup hub, you need a lot of people interested in startups.“解药” 的两个要素 —— 鼓励创业的环境、偶然遇到能提供帮助的人 —— 都源于同一个根本原因：你身边 “创业相关人士” 的数量。要打造一个创业中心，就需要大量对创业感兴趣的人。

There are three reasons. The first, obviously, is that if you don’t have enough density, the chance meetings don’t happen. [4] The second is that different startups need such different things, so you need a lot of people to supply each startup with what they need most. Sean Parker was exactly what Facebook needed in 2004. Another startup might have needed a database guy, or someone with connections in the movie business.原因有三点。第一，很明显，若相关人士密度不够，就不会有那么多偶然相遇的机会。[4] 第二，不同的创业公司需求差异极大，因此需要足够多的人，才能为每家公司提供其最急需的资源。肖恩・帕克正是 2004 年 Facebook 所需要的人，而另一家创业公司可能需要的是数据库专家，或是在电影行业有人脉的人。

This is one of the reasons we fund such a large number of companies, incidentally. The bigger the community, the greater the chance it will contain the person who has that one thing you need most.顺便说一句，这也是我们投资大量公司的原因之一。社群规模越大，其中包含 “能提供你最急需资源的人” 的概率就越高。

The third reason you need a lot of people to make a startup hub is that once you have enough people interested in the same problem, they start to set the social norms. And it is a particularly valuable thing when the atmosphere around you encourages you to do something that would otherwise seem too ambitious. In most places the atmosphere pulls you back toward the mean.需要大量人群才能打造创业中心的第三个原因是：一旦有足够多的人对同一问题（注：指创业）感兴趣，他们就会开始建立社会规范。当周围的氛围鼓励你去做那些原本看似 “过于雄心勃勃” 的事时，这种规范的价值就会凸显。而在多数地区，氛围只会把你拉向 “平庸”。

I flew into the Bay Area a few days ago. I notice this every time I fly over the Valley: somehow you can sense something is going on. Obviously you can sense prosperity in how well kept a place looks. But there are different kinds of prosperity. Silicon Valley doesn’t look like Boston, or New York, or LA, or DC. I tried asking myself what word I’d use to describe the feeling the Valley radiated, and the word that came to mind was optimism.几天前我飞往旧金山湾区（注：硅谷所在区域）。每次飞越硅谷时我都会注意到：你能隐约感觉到这里正在发生一些特别的事。显然，从一个地方的整洁程度能看出其繁荣，但繁荣有不同的类型。硅谷看起来不像波士顿、纽约、洛杉矶或华盛顿特区。我试着问自己，该用哪个词来描述硅谷所散发的氛围，脑海中浮现的词是 “乐观”。

Notes

注释

[1] I’m not saying it’s impossible to succeed in a city with few other startups, just harder. If you’re sufficiently good at generating your own morale, you can survive without external encouragement. Wufoo was based in Tampa and they succeeded. But the Wufoos are exceptionally disciplined.[1] 我并非说在其他创业公司寥寥无几的城市就无法成功，只是难度会更大。如果你足够擅长自我激励，即便没有外部鼓励也能坚持下去。Wufoo（注：知名表单工具公司）的总部位于坦帕，且取得了成功，但 Wufoo 团队拥有极其出色的自律性。

[2] Incidentally, this phenomenon is not limited to startups. Most unusual ambitions fail, unless the person who has them manages to find the right sort of community.[2] 顺便提一句，这种现象并非只存在于创业领域。多数不寻常的远大目标都会以失败告终，除非拥有这些目标的人能找到合适的社群支持。

[3] Starting a company is common, but starting a startup is rare. I’ve talked about the distinction between the two elsewhere, but essentially a startup is a new business designed for scale. Most new businesses are service businesses and except in rare cases those don’t scale.[3] 创办公司很常见，但创办 “创业公司”（startup）却很罕见。我曾在其他地方讨论过二者的区别，本质上，创业公司是一种 “为规模化发展而设计的新业务”。多数新公司都属于服务型企业，除极少数情况外，这类企业难以实现规模化扩张。

[4] As I was writing this, I had a demonstration of the density of startup people in the Valley. Jessica and I bicycled to University Ave in Palo Alto to have lunch at the fabulous Oren’s Hummus. As we walked in, we met Charlie Cheever sitting near the door. Selina Tobaccowala stopped to say hello on her way out. Then Josh Wilson came in to pick up a take out order. After lunch we went to get frozen yogurt. On the way we met Rajat Suri. When we got to the yogurt place, we found Dave Shen there, and as we walked out we ran into Yuri Sagalov. We walked with him for a block or so and we ran into Muzzammil Zaveri, and then a block later we met Aydin Senkut. This is everyday life in Palo Alto. I wasn’t trying to meet people; I was just having lunch. And I’m sure for every startup founder or investor I saw that I knew, there were 5 more I didn’t. If Ron Conway had been with us he would have met 30 people he knew.[4] 在我撰写本文时，恰好有一件事能体现硅谷创业相关人士的密度。我和杰西卡骑自行车到帕洛阿尔托的大学大道，去很棒的 Oren’s Hummus 餐厅吃午饭。进门时，我们看到查理・奇弗（注：知名创业者，曾联合创办 Quora）坐在门边；塞莉娜・托巴科瓦拉（注：科技行业高管、创业者）出门时特意停下来打招呼；之后乔希・威尔逊（注：创业领域人士）进来取外卖。午饭后我们去买冻酸奶，路上遇到了拉贾特・苏里（注：创业者）；到了酸奶店，又碰到戴夫・沈（注：创业领域人士）；出门时撞见了尤里・萨加洛夫（注：创业者）。我们和他一起走了一个街区左右，遇到了穆扎米尔・扎韦里（注：创业领域人士）；再走一个街区，又碰到了艾丁・森库特（注：风投家）。这就是帕洛阿尔托的日常生活 —— 我并没有刻意去见人，只是去吃了个午饭。而且我敢肯定，我遇到的每一个认识的创业公司创始人或投资人背后，还有 5 个我不认识的同行。如果罗恩・康威（注：硅谷知名天使投资人，人脉极广）和我们在一起，他大概能遇到 30 个认识的人。
